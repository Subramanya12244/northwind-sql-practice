# Q01. Product Revenue

**Category:** Aggregations & Revenue Analysis
**Difficulty:** Easy

---

## Problem Statement

The sales analytics team wants a breakdown of revenue contributed by every product in the catalog, after accounting for any discounts applied at the line-item level. This report feeds into product performance reviews and discontinuation decisions.

## Objective

Calculate the total revenue generated by each product across all orders, where revenue is defined as `unit_price * quantity * (1 - discount)`, summed at the product level.

## Tables Used

- `products`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| product_id | Unique identifier of the product |
| product_name | Name of the product |
| total_revenue | Sum of discounted revenue generated by the product across all orders |

**Sample output:**

| product_id | product_name | total_revenue |
|------------|--------------|---------------|
| 38 | Côte de Blaye | 149984.20 |
| 29 | Thüringer Rostbratwurst | 87736.40 |
| 59 | Raclette Courdavault | 76683.75 |
| 60 | Camembert Pierrot | 50286.00 |
| 62 | Tarte au sucre | 47234.97 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- INNER JOIN
- GROUP BY
- Aggregate Functions (SUM)
- Numeric Formatting (ROUND/CAST)

## Why This Approach

**Why `JOIN` instead of a subquery:** `order_details` holds one row per product per order — joining it to `products` lets every line item contribute to its product's running total in a single pass.

**Why `order_details.unit_price`, not `products.unit_price`:** the price in `products` reflects the *current* catalog price, which can change after the sale occurred. `order_details.unit_price` is the price actually charged at the time of that specific transaction — using the wrong one silently produces inaccurate historical revenue.

**Why `GROUP BY` both `product_id` and `product_name`:** PostgreSQL requires every selected non-aggregated column to appear in `GROUP BY`. Grouping by `product_id` alone (the technically sufficient key) would still require `product_name` in the clause since it's in the `SELECT` list.

## Common Mistakes

- Using `products.unit_price` instead of `order_details.unit_price`, which silently produces wrong historical totals if prices changed.
- Forgetting to apply `(1 - discount)`, which overstates revenue — `discount` is stored as a fraction (e.g. `0.15` for 15%), not a flat amount.
- Using `COUNT()` instead of `SUM()` when the intent is revenue, not order/line-item volume.
- Omitting `ROUND()`/casting to `numeric`, which can leave revenue displayed in noisy floating-point form.

## Difficulty

**Easy**

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. Why do we use `order_details.unit_price` instead of `products.unit_price` for revenue calculations?
2. How would this query change if you needed revenue *before* discounts were applied?
3. What would happen to the result if a product had never been ordered — would it appear in this output? Why or why not?
4. How would you modify this query to also show the quantity sold alongside revenue?
5. If `discount` could be `NULL` for some rows, how would that affect the calculation, and how would you defend against it?

## Learning Outcomes

- Understand why transactional (point-in-time) price fields differ from current catalog price fields, and why that distinction matters for historical reporting.
- Practice computing derived metrics (discounted revenue) inside an aggregate function.
- Reinforce the GROUP BY rule that every non-aggregated selected column must be included in the grouping clause.
