# Q02. Category Revenue

**Category:** Aggregations & Revenue Analysis
**Difficulty:** Easy

---

## Problem Statement

Category managers need a consolidated view of how much revenue each product category generates after discounts, to compare category performance and guide merchandising strategy.

## Objective

Calculate total discounted revenue generated by each product category by rolling up product-level sales through the category hierarchy.

## Tables Used

- `categories`
- `products`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| category_id | Unique identifier of the category |
| category_name | Name of the category |
| total_revenue | Sum of discounted revenue generated by all products in the category |

**Sample output:**

| category_id | category_name | total_revenue |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| 1 | Beverages | 267868.18 |
| 4 | Dairy Products | 234507.29 |
| 8 | Seafood | 141623.09 |
| 3 | Confections | 177099.10 |
| 6 | Meat/Poultry | 163022.36 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- INNER JOIN (multi-table)
- GROUP BY
- Aggregate Functions (SUM)

## Why This Approach

**Why three tables are joined:** revenue lives in `order_details`, but category information lives in `categories` — `products` is the bridge table that connects a `product_id` to a `category_id`. There is no way to go directly from `order_details` to `categories` without it.

**Why `INNER JOIN` and not `LEFT JOIN`:** the question asks for revenue *generated*, implying only categories with actual sales activity are relevant. A category with products but zero sales would show `NULL`/`0` either way once aggregated, but `INNER JOIN` is the more natural read here since we're chaining through `order_details`, which only exists for completed line items.

## Common Mistakes

- Joining `order_details` to `categories` directly (skipping `products`) — there's no shared key, so this isn't possible, but it's a common conceptual error for beginners learning the schema.
- Forgetting that one category contains many products — failing to roll up correctly results in revenue tied to the wrong grain.
- Re-using `products.unit_price` again instead of `order_details.unit_price` (same trap as Q1).

## Difficulty

**Easy**

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. Why is the `products` table necessary in this join — couldn't we join `order_details` to `categories` directly?
2. How would you extend this to show each category's revenue as a percentage of total company revenue?
3. What's the difference between aggregating at category level here versus aggregating at product level first and then summing by category? Would results differ?
4. How would you handle a category that exists but has no products at all?

## Learning Outcomes

- Practice multi-table joins where an intermediate 'bridge' table is required to connect two tables that don't share a direct foreign key.
- Reinforce the revenue formula pattern across a different grain (category vs product).
