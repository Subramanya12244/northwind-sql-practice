# Q03. Top 10 Customers by Revenue

**Category:** Aggregations & Revenue Analysis
**Difficulty:** Easy

---

## Problem Statement

The account management team wants to identify the company's most valuable customers by revenue, in order to prioritize relationship management and retention efforts on the accounts that matter most.

## Objective

Identify the 10 customers who have generated the highest total discounted revenue across all their orders.

## Tables Used

- `customers`
- `orders`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| customer_id | Unique identifier of the customer |
| company_name | Name of the customer's company |
| total_revenue | Sum of discounted revenue generated by this customer across all orders |

**Sample output:**

| customer_id | company_name | total_revenue |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| QUICK | QUICK-Stop | 117483.39 |
| ERNSH | Ernst Handel | 104874.98 |
| SAVEA | Save-a-lot Markets | 104361.95 |
| RATTC | Rattlesnake Canyon Grocery | 51097.80 |
| HUNGO | Hungry Owl All-Night Grocers | 49979.91 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- INNER JOIN (multi-table)
- GROUP BY
- Aggregate Functions (SUM)
- ORDER BY
- LIMIT

## Why This Approach

**Why join through `orders`:** `customers` has no direct link to `order_details`; `orders` is the bridge that connects a `customer_id` to the individual line items in `order_details`.

**Why `LIMIT 10` after `ORDER BY ... DESC`:** sorting descending first, then limiting, guarantees the *highest* 10 — limiting before sorting would return an arbitrary 10 rows depending on storage order, which is a common mistake.

## Common Mistakes

- Applying `LIMIT` before fully aggregating (e.g. inside a subquery without `ORDER BY`), which can return an arbitrary top-10 rather than the true top-10 by revenue.
- Not handling ties at the 10th position — `LIMIT 10` will silently cut off ties; if exact tie-handling is required, `RANK()` with a filter on rank `<= 10` is more correct.
- Confusing 'top by revenue' with 'top by number of orders' (see Q5) — these can produce very different customer lists.

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. What's the risk of using `LIMIT` without a preceding `ORDER BY`?
2. If two customers are tied for 10th place by revenue, would this query show both, one, or neither? How would you fix that if the requirement was to show all ties?
3. How would you adapt this query to show the top 10 customers per country instead of overall?
4. What indexes would you expect to help this query perform well on a large orders table?

## Learning Outcomes

- Practice the 'aggregate, then rank/limit' pattern common in business reporting.
- Understand the importance of `ORDER BY` before `LIMIT` for deterministic top-N results.
