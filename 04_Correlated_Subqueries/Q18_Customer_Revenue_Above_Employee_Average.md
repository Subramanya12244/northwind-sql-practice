# Q18. Customer Revenue Above Employee Average

**Category:** Correlated Subqueries
**Difficulty:** Hard

---

## Problem Statement

Sales management wants, for every employee, to identify which of *their* customers generate above-average revenue *relative to that employee's own customer base* — not relative to the company as a whole. This supports per-employee account prioritization.

## Objective

For each employee, return the customers whose revenue (from orders handled by that employee) exceeds the average revenue of all customers handled by that same employee.

## Tables Used

- `orders`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| employee_id | Employee who handled the orders |
| customer_id | Customer whose revenue is being evaluated |
| revenue | Revenue generated by this customer, specifically from orders handled by this employee |

**Sample output:**

| employee_id | customer_id | revenue |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| 4 | QUICK | 28734.90 |
| 4 | SAVEA | 21345.10 |
| 3 | ERNSH | 19872.55 |
| 1 | HUNGO | 15234.40 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- CTE
- Correlated Subquery
- GROUP BY
- Aggregate Functions (SUM, AVG)

## Why This Approach

**Why this is a genuinely correlated subquery, unlike Q15-Q17:** the inner subquery's `WHERE ecr2.employee_id = ecr.employee_id` clause directly references `ecr.employee_id` — a column from the *outer* query's current row. This means the inner query cannot be evaluated once and reused; it must be logically re-evaluated for every distinct employee in the outer result, computing that specific employee's own customer-average each time.

**Why the average is 'per employee', not global:** this is precisely what distinguishes this question from Q6/Q16 — those compare a customer to the *company-wide* average; this compares a customer to the average *within the specific employee's portfolio*. The grain of comparison is fundamentally narrower.

**Why a CTE is used to pre-aggregate `employee_customer_revenue` before applying the correlated filter:** without it, the correlated subquery would need to repeat the full `orders`/`order_details` join and revenue calculation inside the subquery itself, on every outer row — far more verbose and considerably more expensive to execute.

## Common Mistakes

- Comparing against the company-wide average instead of the per-employee average — this collapses the correlation entirely and turns the query into a variant of Q16.
- Forgetting the correlation condition (`ecr2.employee_id = ecr.employee_id`) in the inner subquery, which would make it non-correlated and compute a single global average instead of one per employee.
- Not pre-aggregating revenue in a CTE first, leading to a much more expensive and harder-to-read query that recomputes joins inside the correlated subquery.

## Difficulty

**Hard**

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. What makes this subquery correlated, specifically? Point to the exact clause.
2. How does the average computed in this query differ conceptually from the average computed in Q6?
3. Why does pre-aggregating into a CTE before applying the correlated filter help performance, even though the subquery is still technically correlated?
4. How would you rewrite this using a window function (e.g. `AVG(...) OVER (PARTITION BY employee_id)`) instead of a correlated subquery? What would change?
5. If an employee has only one customer, would that customer ever appear in this result? Why or why not?

## Learning Outcomes

- Understand the precise technical definition of a correlated subquery, and the difference between 'above the global average' and 'above this entity's own group average'.
- Practice combining a CTE (for cheap pre-aggregation) with a correlated subquery (for per-group comparison) — a powerful and common real-world pattern.
- Recognize when window functions could replace a correlated subquery for better performance and readability.
