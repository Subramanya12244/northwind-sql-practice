# Q16. Customers Above Average Revenue (Correlated Subquery)

**Category:** Correlated Subqueries
**Difficulty:** Medium

---

## Problem Statement

Same business need as Q6 — flag above-average-revenue customers — solved via `HAVING` and a nested subquery rather than a CTE, for comparison.

## Objective

Return customers whose total revenue exceeds the average revenue across all customers, using a `HAVING` clause referencing a subquery.

## Tables Used

- `customers`
- `orders`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| customer_id | Unique identifier of the customer |
| company_name | Name of the customer's company |
| total_revenue | Sum of discounted revenue generated by this customer |

**Sample output:**

| customer_id | company_name | total_revenue |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| QUICK | QUICK-Stop | 117483.39 |
| ERNSH | Ernst Handel | 104874.98 |
| SAVEA | Save-a-lot Markets | 104361.95 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- INNER JOIN (multi-table)
- GROUP BY
- HAVING
- Subquery (non-correlated)
- Aggregate Functions (SUM, AVG)

## Why This Approach

**Why the revenue expression must be repeated identically in both the outer `HAVING` and the inner derived table:** SQL doesn't let you reference an outer `SELECT`'s computed alias inside `HAVING`'s own comparison value in this structure — the inner subquery has to independently recompute customer revenue from scratch (joining `orders` and `order_details` again) to produce the company-wide average, since it's logically a separate, self-contained calculation over a different grouping scope (all customers) than the outer query's current group (one customer).

**Trade-off versus the CTE version (Q6):** this version recomputes the same revenue logic twice in the query text, which is more error-prone to maintain (if the revenue formula changes, it must be updated in two places) — this is one of the strongest practical arguments for preferring CTEs in real production code.

## Common Mistakes

- Letting the revenue formula drift out of sync between the outer `HAVING` and the inner subquery — e.g. fixing a discount bug in one but not the other.
- Forgetting `HAVING` (not `WHERE`) is required since the filter target is an aggregate.

**Medium**

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. What's the main maintenance risk of this subquery approach compared to the CTE version in Q6?
2. Why must the revenue calculation be duplicated here, rather than referencing the outer query's computed total directly?
3. Which version would you choose in a production codebase, and why?
4. How would query performance compare between this version and the CTE version, in principle?

## Learning Outcomes

- Recognize the real-world maintainability cost of duplicated logic in subquery-heavy SQL versus CTE-based SQL.
- Reinforce when `HAVING` plus a subquery is an appropriate (if less elegant) alternative to a CTE.
