# Q48. Suppliers and Total Revenue

**Category:** RIGHT JOIN
**Difficulty:** Medium

---

## Problem Statement

The procurement team wants a report showing every supplier and the total revenue generated from the products they supply. Suppliers with no product sales should also appear with a revenue of 0.

## Objective

Return all suppliers along with the total revenue generated by their products, ensuring suppliers with no sales appear with 0.00.

## Tables Used

- `order_details`
- `products`
- `suppliers`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| company_name | Name of the supplier |
| total_revenue | Total discounted revenue from products supplied by this supplier (0.00 if no sales) |

**Sample output:**

| company_name | total_revenue |
|--------------|---------------|
| Plutzer Lebensmittelgro√üm√§rkte AG | 74906.01 |
| Exotic Liquids | 24567.80 |
| Supplier X | 0.00 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset.)*

## Concepts Used

- RIGHT JOIN (chained, two levels)
- GROUP BY
- Aggregate Functions (SUM)
- NULL Handling (COALESCE)
- ROUND / CAST

## Why This Approach

**Why the chain goes `order_details ‚Üí products ‚Üí suppliers`:** revenue lives in `order_details`, but there is no direct link between `order_details` and `suppliers`. The path requires two hops: `order_details.product_id ‚Üí products.product_id ‚Üí products.supplier_id ‚Üí suppliers.supplier_id`. Two joins are needed, and both must be `RIGHT JOIN` to preserve `suppliers` (the final right table) all the way through.

**Understanding the two-level RIGHT JOIN chain:**
- First `RIGHT JOIN`: `order_details` (left) RIGHT JOIN `products` (right) ‚Äî preserves all products, including those never ordered.
- Second `RIGHT JOIN`: `[order_details + products]` (left) RIGHT JOIN `suppliers` (right) ‚Äî preserves all suppliers, including those whose products have never been ordered.

The net result: every supplier appears, regardless of whether their products have ever been sold.

**Why `COALESCE` is required:** `SUM()` returns NULL for suppliers whose products have no matching `order_details` rows. `COALESCE` converts that NULL to `0.00` as required.

**LEFT JOIN equivalent (same result, arguably clearer):**
```sql
SELECT
    s.company_name,
    COALESCE(ROUND(SUM(od.quantity * od.unit_price * (1 - od.discount))::numeric, 2), 0) AS total_revenue
FROM suppliers s
LEFT JOIN products p ON p.supplier_id = s.supplier_id
LEFT JOIN order_details od ON od.product_id = p.product_id
GROUP BY s.company_name, s.supplier_id;
```

**Why `GROUP BY s.company_name, s.supplier_id`:** `supplier_id` is the unique key; `company_name` is included as a non-aggregated selected column. Grouping on `company_name` alone risks merging two suppliers with identical names.

## Common Mistakes

- Reversing the chain direction ‚Äî placing `suppliers` on the left and `order_details` on the right with `RIGHT JOIN` at both steps would preserve `order_details`, not `suppliers`.
- Converting either `RIGHT JOIN` to `INNER JOIN`, which drops suppliers with no product sales at that step.
- Forgetting `COALESCE` ‚Äî `SUM()` returns NULL (not 0) for empty groups.
- Grouping by `company_name` alone, risking silent row merges for suppliers with identical names.

## Interview Follow-up Questions

**1. Why does the chain go `order_details ‚Üí products ‚Üí suppliers` and not `suppliers ‚Üí products ‚Üí order_details`?**

With `RIGHT JOIN`, the table you want to fully preserve must be the rightmost table in the final join. Since the requirement is to preserve all suppliers, `suppliers` must be on the right. Working backwards: `products` is the bridge between `order_details` and `suppliers`, so it sits in the middle. The chain reads right-to-left in terms of "what's being preserved at each step": first `products` (relative to `order_details`), then `suppliers` (relative to the combined result). If you were using `LEFT JOIN` instead, the chain would read left-to-right in the more natural order: `suppliers ‚Üí products ‚Üí order_details`.

**2. What does the first RIGHT JOIN (order_details ‚Üí products) preserve, and why does it matter?**

The first `RIGHT JOIN` preserves all products, including those with no `order_details` rows (never-ordered products). This matters because a supplier could have products in the catalog that have never been ordered ‚Äî those products must survive into the second join so their supplier can still appear in the final result. If the first join were `INNER JOIN`, never-ordered products would be dropped, and their suppliers would then have no rows to carry forward ‚Äî potentially disappearing from the result even though the second `RIGHT JOIN` is trying to preserve them.

**3. Is there a scenario where `company_name` alone would be an unsafe GROUP BY key?**

Yes ‚Äî if two different suppliers happen to have the same `company_name`. This is more common than it seems in real-world supplier data (e.g. regional branches of the same company registered separately). Grouping on `company_name` alone would silently merge their rows and combine their revenue figures into one row, producing incorrect totals. Always include the primary key (`supplier_id`) in `GROUP BY` alongside display columns.

**4. How would you extend this to also show the number of distinct products each supplier contributes?**

Add `COUNT(DISTINCT p.product_id)` to the `SELECT`. Since `products` is already in the join chain (as the intermediate table), `p.product_id` is available without any additional joins:

```sql
SELECT
    s.company_name,
    COUNT(DISTINCT p.product_id) AS product_count,
    COALESCE(ROUND(SUM(od.quantity * od.unit_price * (1 - od.discount))::numeric, 2), 0) AS total_revenue
FROM order_details od
RIGHT JOIN products p ON p.product_id = od.product_id
RIGHT JOIN suppliers s ON s.supplier_id = p.supplier_id
GROUP BY s.company_name, s.supplier_id
ORDER BY total_revenue DESC;
```

**5. How would you find the supplier with the highest average revenue per product?**

Use a CTE to compute total revenue and product count per supplier, then divide:

```sql
WITH supplier_stats AS (
    SELECT
        s.company_name,
        s.supplier_id,
        COALESCE(SUM(od.quantity * od.unit_price * (1 - od.discount)), 0) AS total_revenue,
        COUNT(DISTINCT p.product_id) AS product_count
    FROM order_details od
    RIGHT JOIN products p ON p.product_id = od.product_id
    RIGHT JOIN suppliers s ON s.supplier_id = p.supplier_id
    GROUP BY s.company_name, s.supplier_id
)
SELECT
    company_name,
    ROUND((total_revenue / NULLIF(product_count, 0))::numeric, 2) AS avg_revenue_per_product
FROM supplier_stats
ORDER BY avg_revenue_per_product DESC NULLS LAST;
```

`NULLIF(product_count, 0)` guards against division by zero for suppliers with no products.

## Learning Outcomes

- Understand how chained RIGHT JOINs work when three tables are involved ‚Äî the preserved entity is the rightmost, and each intermediate join must also be RIGHT to avoid dropping rows before the final join.
- Recognise that the RIGHT JOIN chain `A ‚Üí B ‚Üí C` is the exact mirror of the LEFT JOIN chain `C ‚Üí B ‚Üí A` ‚Äî same logic, opposite table ordering.
- Reinforce that `SUM()` always needs `COALESCE` in outer join aggregation contexts, regardless of join direction.
