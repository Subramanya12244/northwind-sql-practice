# Q06. Customers Above Average Revenue

**Category:** GROUP BY & HAVING
**Difficulty:** Medium

---

## Problem Statement

Finance wants to flag customers whose lifetime revenue exceeds the average revenue across all customers, to separate above-average accounts from the long tail for targeted upsell campaigns.

## Objective

Return all customers whose total revenue is strictly greater than the average total revenue computed across all customers.

## Tables Used

- `customers`
- `orders`
- `order_details`

## Expected Output

| Column Name | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| customer_id | Unique identifier of the customer |
| company_name | Name of the customer's company |
| total_revenue | Sum of discounted revenue generated by this customer |

**Sample output:**

| customer_id | company_name | total_revenue |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| QUICK | QUICK-Stop | 117483.39 |
| ERNSH | Ernst Handel | 104874.98 |
| SAVEA | Save-a-lot Markets | 104361.95 |
| RATTC | Rattlesnake Canyon Grocery | 51097.80 |
| HUNGO | Hungry Owl All-Night Grocers | 49979.91 |

*(Sample values are illustrative, based on the standard Northwind dataset, and intended to show shape/format — not guaranteed to match your exact data instance.)*

## Concepts Used

- INNER JOIN
- GROUP BY
- CTE
- Aggregate Functions (SUM, AVG)
- Subquery

## Why This Approach

**Why a CTE is needed instead of plugging straight into `HAVING`:** `HAVING SUM(revenue) > AVG(SUM(revenue))` looks intuitive but doesn't work — `AVG()` over `SUM()` would just average the *current group's* single revenue value (itself), not the average across *all* customer groups. The average must be computed independently, across the full set of per-customer totals, which requires materializing those totals first (via a CTE or subquery) before comparing.

**Why the CTE is referenced twice:** once to list the rows, and once inside a scalar subquery to compute `AVG(total_revenue)` over the *entire* CTE result set — independent of any single customer's row.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing `HAVING SUM(revenue) > AVG(revenue)` directly, expecting it to compare against the global average — it doesn't; `AVG()` here would just equal `SUM()` for a single group of one value.
- Forgetting to recompute revenue identically in both halves (the CTE and the comparison) which can introduce subtle mismatches in more complex variants.
- Treating 'average customer revenue' as 'average order revenue' — these are very different denominators (one is per-customer, the other is per-order).

## Difficulty

**Medium**

## Interview Follow-up Questions

1. Why can't you just write `HAVING SUM(revenue) > AVG(revenue)` to solve this in one pass?
2. What's the difference between this CTE-based approach and a correlated subquery approach (see Q16) for the same problem?
3. How would the result change if you used `AVG(revenue)` computed from `order_details` directly, rather than from per-customer aggregated totals?
4. How would you find customers within, say, 10% of the average rather than strictly above it?

## Learning Outcomes

- Understand why 'above average' comparisons require two passes: one to aggregate per entity, one to aggregate across entities.
- Learn the CTE pattern for materializing an intermediate result set that's then both displayed and used in a comparison.
